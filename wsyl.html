In this tool, monthly humidity is calculated by comparing a city's average dew point to its average temperature. The result of this approximates humidity in the morning; humidity is ordinarily lower than this in the afternoon (by up to 30 points!) and higher at night. A monthly average below 40 on this scale is typical of a desert, the 40s and 50s are dry, the 60s are moderate, the 70s are fairly humid, and anything above 80 is considered very humid.

Desert climate is very dry all year round, the kind of climate one might find in Las Vegas or Baghdad.

Arid climate represents a desert or otherwise dry climate where there is some moisture in the air, often due to being adjacent to an ocean.

Dry steppe climate features snowy or rainy winters and dry, dusty summers. It is common in places like Mongolia and Kazakhstan.

Moderate steppe climate is similar to dry steppe climate, but slightly wetter in the summer. The North American plains are like this.

Mediterranean climate represents the weather conditions common around the Mediterranean Sea: dry in the summer and moderate in the winter.

Monsoon climate has a dry spring, wet summer and moderate winter, so that humidity is relatively uncorrelated with temperature.

Dry winter, humid summer climate is exactly what it sounds like. It is common in the southeastern United States and southern China.

Vancouver Island, at least the east coast of it, is very rainy and foggy in the winter and has Mediterranean or steppe-like conditions in the summer.

Oceanic climate is rainy all year, but especially so in the winter, and typically mild as a result. Great Britain is a good example.

Tropical rainforest climate is also wet all year. Unlike oceanic climate, a tropical rainforest has little variation in humidity throughout the year.
